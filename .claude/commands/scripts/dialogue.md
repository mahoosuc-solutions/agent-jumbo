# Dialogue Generator

Character-driven dialogue with voice consistency, subtext, and pacing.

## Overview

Generate authentic, character-specific dialogue that sounds natural when spoken aloud while serving story purposes. Create conversations with distinct voices, emotional subtext, conflict, and thematic resonance. Build dialogue that reveals character through word choice, rhythm, and what remains unsaid.

### ROI Calculation: $45,000/year

- **Writing Speed**: 12 hours/week saved on dialogue revision ($30,000/year)
- **Character Consistency**: Eliminate voice mixing across characters ($8,000/year)
- **Subtext Integration**: Faster implementation of layered meaning ($5,000/year)
- **Conflict Development**: Improved tension through dialogue beats ($2,000/year)

### Key Benefits

1. **Distinct Character Voices** - Each character speaks with unique patterns and vocabulary
2. **Natural Speech Patterns** - Dialogue sounds authentic when read aloud
3. **Subtext Layering** - Characters say one thing while meaning another
4. **Conflict Escalation** - Build tension through verbal sparring and disagreement
5. **Economy of Language** - Every line serves multiple purposes
6. **Emotional Authenticity** - Dialogue reveals genuine feeling through indirect expression
7. **Pacing Control** - Varied sentence length and rhythm for dramatic effect

## Implementation Steps

### Step 1: Define Character Voice Parameters

Establish unique voice characteristics for each character to ensure consistent differentiation.

**Sub-steps:**

- Identify character background (education, region, profession)
- Determine vocabulary level and sophistication
- Establish speech patterns (formal vs. casual, direct vs. indirect)
- Define verbal tics or repeated phrases
- Note grammatical quirks or dialect features
- Determine sentence length preferences (short and punchy vs. long and flowing)
- Identify topics character discusses vs. avoids
- Establish emotional expression style (demonstrative vs. reserved)
- Note humor style (sarcastic, wholesome, dark, dry)
- Define relationship to truth-telling (honest, evasive, manipulative)
- Establish pace of speech (rapid-fire vs. deliberate)
- Identify cultural or generational language markers
- Note professional jargon or specialized vocabulary
- Define comfort with silence and pauses
- Establish interruption patterns (does character interrupt or get interrupted)
- Determine use of metaphor and abstraction vs. literal speech

### Step 2: Establish Scene Context

Define the situational parameters that will shape dialogue content and emotional tone.

**Sub-steps:**

- Identify scene objective for each character
- Determine power dynamics between speakers
- Establish emotional stakes for each participant
- Define physical environment and how it affects conversation
- Note time pressure or urgency factors
- Identify secrets each character is hiding
- Determine what each character wants from the other
- Establish obstacles to clear communication
- Define relationship history between characters
- Note recent events affecting emotional states
- Identify external threats or pressures
- Determine level of privacy (who might overhear)
- Establish social constraints (professional setting vs. intimate)
- Note physical states (exhaustion, intoxication, illness)
- Define interruption possibilities (phone calls, other people)
- Establish scene's position in overall story arc

### Step 3: Build Dialogue with Subtext

Create conversation where surface meaning differs from underlying intention.

**Sub-steps:**

- Identify what character wants to communicate directly
- Determine what character wants to hide or deflect
- Create surface conversation that seems innocent or casual
- Layer underneath meaning that contradicts surface
- Use word choice to hint at hidden agenda
- Employ metaphor to discuss sensitive topics indirectly
- Create double meanings that reveal character awareness
- Use questions to avoid making statements
- Employ deflection and topic changes
- Create mismatched responses (answering different question)
- Use tone indicators (parentheticals) sparingly for clarity
- Build tension between what's said and what's meant
- Create dramatic irony (audience knows more than characters)
- Use callback references to earlier conversations
- Employ silence and pauses as meaningful responses
- Reveal subtext gradually through accumulation

### Step 4: Craft Conflict and Tension

Develop dialogue that creates or escalates disagreement and dramatic friction.

**Sub-steps:**

- Identify fundamental disagreement between characters
- Start with mild disagreement or different perspectives
- Escalate gradually through conversation progression
- Use interruptions to show mounting frustration
- Employ increasingly sharp word choices
- Shorten sentence length as tension rises
- Use questions as attacks or challenges
- Create misunderstandings that compound conflict
- Employ loaded language with emotional resonance
- Build to breaking point or revelation
- Use silence as aggressive response
- Create power shifts through dialogue control
- Employ repetition to show obsession or desperation
- Use volume indicators (shouting) only when critical
- Allow conflict to reveal character values
- Resolve, intensify, or table conflict based on scene needs

### Step 5: Implement Speech Patterns

Apply authentic speech rhythms and patterns that differentiate characters.

**Sub-steps:**

- Vary sentence length according to character type
- Use contractions for casual or emotional speech
- Avoid contractions for formal or controlled speech
- Employ fragments for naturalistic dialogue
- Use run-on sentences for stream-of-consciousness moments
- Create verbal hesitations (um, uh, well) sparingly
- Implement repetition for emphasis or uncertainty
- Use trailing off (...) for uncomfortable subjects
- Employ interruption markers (--) for overlapping speech
- Create rhythm through word choice and placement
- Use alliteration or assonance for memorable lines
- Vary response length between characters
- Implement pauses (beat) for dramatic effect
- Create call-and-response patterns between characters
- Use question-answer rhythms to build pace
- Employ rhetorical questions for different effect than genuine questions

### Step 6: Infuse Emotion Without Stating It

Show emotional states through dialogue content rather than telling via parentheticals.

**Sub-steps:**

- Choose words that reflect emotional state
- Vary syntax based on emotional intensity
- Use humor or deflection to mask vulnerability
- Employ aggression when character feels threatened
- Create over-explanation when character is anxious
- Use monosyllabic responses for shutdown or anger
- Employ rambling for nervousness
- Create precision in language for control-seeking
- Use metaphor to express difficult emotions
- Employ storytelling to avoid direct emotional admission
- Create topic avoidance around painful subjects
- Use intellectualization to distance from feeling
- Employ blame-shifting for guilt or shame
- Create defensiveness through counter-accusations
- Use inappropriate humor for discomfort
- Allow vulnerability to slip through character's defenses

### Step 7: Balance Exposition and Naturalism

Convey necessary information while maintaining authentic conversational flow.

**Sub-steps:**

- Identify information audience needs to learn
- Determine what characters would realistically discuss
- Break exposition into smaller pieces across multiple exchanges
- Use disagreement to surface different perspectives on facts
- Employ questions to introduce information naturally
- Create reasons for characters to review known information
- Use newcomer character to justify explanation
- Employ skepticism to force re-explanation
- Interrupt exposition with emotional reactions
- Layer exposition with character development
- Use physical action to break up information dumps
- Create urgency that justifies rapid information exchange
- Employ partial information and gradual revelation
- Use misunderstanding to clarify facts through correction
- Integrate exposition with conflict
- Allow some information to remain implied rather than stated

### Step 8: Create Memorable Lines

Craft specific dialogue moments that resonate and remain quotable.

**Sub-steps:**

- Identify key thematic moments for memorable lines
- Use specific, concrete language over generic
- Create unexpected word combinations
- Employ rhythm and cadence for memorability
- Use metaphor that's fresh but comprehensible
- Create contradiction or paradox
- Employ reversal of expectations
- Use rule of three for rhythm
- Create parallel structure for emphasis
- Employ understatement for dramatic effect
- Use overstatement for comedic effect
- Create callback to earlier line with new meaning
- Employ cultural or literary allusion (carefully)
- Use single-word responses for impact
- Create lines that work as act/scene endings
- Test lines by speaking aloud for natural flow

### Step 9: Format Dialogue Properly

Apply correct screenplay or script formatting conventions for professional presentation.

**Sub-steps:**

- Center character name above dialogue in ALL CAPS
- Indent dialogue block appropriately
- Keep dialogue blocks under 5 lines when possible
- Use parentheticals only for essential direction
- Place parentheticals between character name and dialogue
- Limit parentheticals to brief phrases
- Avoid emotional adverbs in parentheticals (angrily, sadly)
- Use (CONT'D) when action interrupts dialogue
- Employ (beat) or (pause) for critical timing only
- Format (V.O.) for voice-over narration
- Use (O.S.) for off-screen dialogue
- Format (PRE-LAP) for dialogue before scene change
- Use -- for interrupted dialogue
- Employ ... for trailing off
- Break long speeches into multiple blocks with action
- Use dual dialogue format for simultaneous speaking

### Step 10: Revise for Speakability

Refine dialogue to ensure it sounds natural when performed aloud.

**Sub-steps:**

- Read all dialogue aloud at performance speed
- Eliminate tongue-twisters and awkward phrasings
- Reduce overly complex sentence structures
- Remove unnatural formality unless character-appropriate
- Cut unnecessary words and redundancy
- Verify contractions sound natural
- Check that questions sound like actual questions
- Ensure exclamations feel genuine
- Test that pauses fall in natural places
- Verify rhythm matches character emotional state
- Check that vocabulary suits character education/background
- Ensure dialect or accent features are subtle
- Remove written-language constructions (however, therefore)
- Test that joke timing works when spoken
- Verify emotional builds feel authentic
- Confirm final versions with table read if possible

## Usage Examples

### Example 1: Dramatic Confrontation

```bash
/dialogue --characters "Sarah,Tom" --tone dramatic --context "Sarah confronts husband Tom about suspicious credit card charges"
```

**Input:** Characters are married 10 years. Sarah is direct and analytical. Tom is evasive and charming. High emotional stakes.

**Output:**

```text
SARAH

TOM
(barely looking up from phone)

SARAH

TOM

SARAH
There's a charge for eight hundred dollars
from a jewelry store.

TOM
(still not looking up)

SARAH

was in April, Tom. That was six months ago.

TOM
(finally looking at her)
Can we talk about this later? I'm in the
middle of something.

SARAH
No. We're talking about it now.
```

## Best Practices

### 1. Listen to Real Conversations

Study how people actually talk - the fragments, interruptions, topic changes, and non-sequiturs. Real speech isn't polished. It's messy, indirect, and often illogical. Use those patterns selectively to create authenticity without sacrificing clarity.

### 2. What Characters Don't Say Matters

The most powerful dialogue often involves what characters refuse to discuss. Create "elephant in the room" conversations where everyone knows the real topic but discusses everything else. Tension builds from avoidance.

### 3. Conflict Doesn't Require Anger

Characters can be in complete disagreement while remaining civil, even warm. Some of the most effective conflict comes from characters who genuinely like each other but fundamentally disagree. Explore conflict beyond shouting matches.

### 4. Use Specificity for Authenticity

Generic dialogue is forgettable. "I'm going to the store" becomes "I'm running to Safeway for milk" - the specific detail creates reality. Characters should use brand names, real places, specific references that ground them in a believable world.

### 5. Rhythm Creates Emotion

Fast-paced, short exchanges create urgency or comedy. Longer, flowing sentences create intimacy or contemplation. Mix rhythms within scenes to control emotional journey. Read dialogue aloud to feel the rhythm.

### 6. Interruption Reveals Relationship

Who interrupts whom tells you everything about power dynamics. Track interruption patterns carefully. Characters who never finish sentences may be submissive, anxious, or constantly cut off. Characters who finish others' sentences may be intimate or domineering.

### 7. Silence Is Dialogue

Well-placed pauses and silences can be more powerful than words. Use (beat) and (pause) sparingly, but when you do, make them count. Silence forces characters and audience to sit with uncomfortable truths.

### 8. Every Character Wants Something

Even in casual conversation, characters have objectives. The person making small talk might be avoiding difficult topics. The person asking questions might be interrogating. Layer wants beneath surface pleasantries.

### 9. Avoid Therapy Speak

Characters rarely articulate emotions with therapeutic precision unless that's specifically their voice. "I feel angry when you dismiss my feelings" is therapy language. "You're doing it again" or "Why do you always--" is how people actually speak in conflict.

## Common Use Cases

### Character Introduction

Use first dialogue to establish personality, background, and relationships. First lines should be memorable and revealing. Create immediate distinction from other characters.

### Exposition Delivery

Convey necessary plot information through character conflict, questions, or disagreements. Never create scenes solely for exposition. Layer information with character development.

### Relationship Development

Show relationship evolution through changing dialogue patterns. Early formality gives way to intimacy, or vice versa. Track power shifts through who controls conversation.

### Comedic Timing

Create humor through misunderstanding, wordplay, unexpected responses, and character-specific perspective. Build jokes through dialogue rhythm and callback references.

### Dramatic Confrontation

Build tension through escalating conflict, interruption patterns, and what characters refuse to say. Create catharsis through breakthrough moments of honesty.

### Romantic Chemistry

Develop attraction through subtext, playful banter, vulnerability moments, and gradually lowering emotional defenses. Show connection through dialogue rhythm and completion patterns.

### Professional Settings

Balance authentic workplace language with accessibility. Use jargon sparingly and define through context. Show hierarchy through formality levels and interruption patterns.
